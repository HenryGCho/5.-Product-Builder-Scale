Capability 01

Opportunity Identification

Synthesising what you have learned into a clear conviction about where the real opportunity sits — the gap, the problem worth solving, and why now.

Insight synthesis Combining customer, market, and domain signals into a single, defensible point of view.
Opportunity framing Articulating the gap clearly enough that others immediately understand the bet.
Problem definition Narrowing from a broad space to the specific problem worth solving first.
L1 Absent
Has a product idea but no synthesis of why this problem is worth solving over others — discovery inputs exist as separate notes without a clear takeaway. The problem being solved shifts depending on who last asked about it.
L2 Emerging
Can describe the problem and point to some evidence, but the opportunity framing is broad enough to mean several different things. Multiple problems compete for attention without a clear conviction about which one is the real prize.
L3 Developing Baseline
Has synthesised discovery into a clear problem statement with supporting evidence — the gap, who it affects most, and why now. The framing is reasonably stable but can shift under sustained questioning or when significant new information arrives.
L4 Strong
Holds a sharp, evidence-backed conviction about the specific opportunity — precise enough that a new team member could understand the strategic bet within minutes. The framing is stable under pressure and creates immediate alignment when shared.
L5 Exceptional
Frames opportunities in ways that redefine how others see a market or problem space. Has a track record of identifying and articulating opportunities that proved correct before they were widely recognised — the framing itself becomes a reference point others respond to.
Capability 02

North Star Experience

Defining and communicating the future state compellingly — the product experience, the design ambition, and the innovative leap that makes it worth building.

Vision crafting Translating conviction into a vivid, specific picture of what the product experience should feel like.
Experience design Designing the key moments and interactions that define what 'good' looks like for the customer.
Narrative design Communicating the vision in a form that aligns a team without requiring constant interpretation.
L1 Absent
The product vision is defined by a feature list or functional spec with no picture of what the future experience actually feels like for the customer. Different team members are building toward different mental models of success.
L2 Emerging
Can describe a desired future state in general terms — "seamless," "powerful," "delightful" — but the vision isn't specific enough to make design decisions obvious or align a team without verbal interpretation each time.
L3 Developing Baseline
Has a concrete picture of the target experience — specific moments, emotions, and interactions that define success. The vision guides major decisions but isn't yet communicated compellingly enough to operate without the founder's active involvement in key calls.
L4 Strong
The north star experience is vivid, specific, and stable — expressed in multiple formats (narrative, prototype, reference examples) and shared clearly enough that others make the right calls without constant input. Trade-offs become obvious when evaluated against it.
L5 Exceptional
Creates visions that redefine what "good" looks like in a category — their articulation of the target experience becomes the benchmark others respond to or build against. Has a track record of visions that proved directionally right and sustained the work required to reach them.
Capability 03

Business Model

The commercial logic that makes the opportunity viable — how value is created, delivered, and captured in a way that sustains and scales.

Business model design Structuring how value flows from creation through delivery to capture.
Value proposition Making explicit what the customer gets that they can't get elsewhere, and why they'd pay for it.
Revenue modelling Building and stress-testing the financial logic that underpins the commercial model.
L1 Absent
Business model is an assumption — "we'll charge a subscription" — without modelling what drives unit economics or how value is actually captured. Revenue thinking hasn't been examined under any real scrutiny.
L2 Emerging
Can articulate how the business makes money and name the value proposition. Has done some revenue modelling but key assumptions — conversion rates, retention, willingness to pay — are largely guesses rather than evidence-based estimates.
L3 Developing Baseline
Has a coherent, reasoned business model with explicit assumptions and a basic model of unit economics. Has validated at least some key assumptions through real customer conversations or early transactions. Can explain what needs to be true for the model to work — and what would break it.
L4 Strong
Maintains a live model of the business — pricing, value prop, unit economics — grounded in real data and updated as the business evolves. Can identify which assumptions are load-bearing, knows which haven't been tested yet, and is actively designing experiments to stress-test them.
L5 Exceptional
Has designed or pioneered business models that changed the economics of a category — or demonstrated rare ability to find non-obvious commercial structures in new spaces. Their business model thinking is studied or publicly referenced by others in the field.
Capability 04

Delivery Plan

The sequencing, prioritisation and resourcing of how you get from here to the north star — ruthless about what comes first and why.

Prioritisation Choosing what to build first based on strategic logic, not urgency or volume of requests.
Roadmapping Sequencing work into a plan that connects near-term execution to the longer-term vision.
Resource planning Matching ambition to realistic capacity across people, agents, and time.
L1 Absent
The roadmap is a wishlist with no clear prioritisation logic — features are added as ideas arrive and nothing is ever removed. Resource allocation is reactive, shaped by whoever last raised a concern rather than by strategic logic.
L2 Emerging
Has a sequenced plan that distinguishes near-term from longer-term, but prioritisation criteria aren't explicit or consistently applied. The plan is aspirational — it doesn't fully account for available resources or realistic timelines.
L3 Developing Baseline
Maintains a roadmap with explicit prioritisation logic tied to the opportunity and north star. Can explain what comes first and why, and has resource-balanced the near-term plan against realistic capacity. The plan adapts when new information arrives but doesn't change shape with every conversation.
L4 Strong
The delivery plan is a live strategic document — sequenced, resource-constrained, and explicitly connected to the business model and north star. Trade-offs are named, not hidden. Any stakeholder can understand what's being bet on and why the sequence is right.
L5 Exceptional
Has developed delivery planning approaches others adopt — for managing radical uncertainty, for coordinating human and agent work at speed, or for maintaining strategic coherence while moving fast. Their sequencing or prioritisation frameworks are referenced as models by others.
Capability 05

Operating Model agent-native

Strategic decisions about how humans and agents divide the work of envisioning and building — what requires human judgment and what can be delegated.

AI workflow design Designing repeatable processes where agents contribute reliably as part of the delivery system.
Delegation design Deciding explicitly which tasks require human judgment and which can be handed to agents.
Human-AI teaming Structuring collaboration between people and agents so each contributes what they do best.
L1 Absent
Work is allocated based on habit — humans do what they've always done, agents (if used) receive ad hoc tasks with no deliberate design. No thinking has happened about which work genuinely requires human judgment and which can be delegated.
L2 Emerging Baseline
Aware that agents can take on more work and has started experimenting with delegation. But the division of work has not been systematically designed — it has evolved through trial and error with no explicit principles about what stays human.
L3 Developing Target
Has made explicit decisions about how humans and agents divide the work of building — which tasks require human taste, judgment, or accountability, and which can be fully or partially delegated. These decisions are documented, even if the model is still evolving.
L4 Strong
Maintains a coherent operating model — clear about which work requires human judgment, which benefits from human-agent collaboration, and which can run autonomously. The model is reviewed and updated as agent capabilities improve; workflow design is a strategic activity, not an afterthought.
L5 Exceptional
Has developed original frameworks for human-AI work design that others adopt. Can design an operating model for a new team or domain quickly and precisely — their approach to delegation and human-agent teaming is referenced as a model for agent-native product organisations.
