Understand

Five dimensions that define how well a founder understands their customers, market, and operating context. Each level describes what competence looks like in practice — concrete enough to self-assess honestly.

Dimension 01

Customer Insight

Customer discovery Persona development Demand unfolding
L1 Absent
Pain points are assumed or described in product terms — "users need a faster export" rather than "users are losing clients because reports arrive too late." Has not done structured discovery; personas are demographic templates invented in a workshop or copied from a brief.
L2 Emerging
Has spoken to some customers but interviews tend to confirm existing beliefs rather than challenge them. Can describe who the customer is but struggles to articulate what they're actually trying to accomplish — and why current solutions, including yours, fall short.
L3 Developing Baseline
Runs recurring interviews with a loose framework and can articulate what customers are trying to accomplish beyond the surface request — "close books faster," not "better UI." Personas exist and are referenced in planning, but a competitor launch or a pricing conversation can still bypass them entirely.
L4 Strong
Maintains a record of customer evidence — updated after every significant discovery session — that connects pain points to underlying goals to demand signals. When a competitor ships a feature, you already know from your interviews whether it matters to your segment; customer insight shapes what gets built, what gets cut, and how you position.
L5 Exceptional
Has developed discovery methods others in the domain adopt — published, taught, or publicly referenced. Can surface demand that customers themselves can't articulate, has made product bets based on that, and the bets held up; their process, not just their instincts, is what others seek to replicate.
Dimension 02

Strategic Landscape

Strategic analysis Competitive positioning Macro analysis
L1 Absent
Strategy is reactive and internal-facing — decisions are driven by what's buildable or what a customer asked for last week. Cannot explain the company's strategic bets in one sentence or connect a product decision back to them.
L2 Emerging Baseline
Can describe the company strategy at a high level but can't reliably connect it to specific product choices. Reads macro analysis when it surfaces — the Sequoia macro deck, an a16z essay — but it rarely changes what's being built.
L3 Developing
Conducts periodic strategic reviews and can articulate how macro shifts — rising AI infrastructure costs, a new regulatory regime, a platform consolidation — are affecting the market. Competitive positioning is defined but reviewed infrequently; a gap remains between strategic awareness and what gets prioritised week to week.
L4 Strong
Maintains a coherent strategic picture that links macro conditions → market position → product bets, and can explain each link explicitly. Can articulate not just what is being built but why now — what external window makes this the right moment — and can name what has been deliberately not built, and why.
L5 Exceptional
Has published or publicly articulated a view of where the market is heading that others cite and build strategies around. Has a track record of framing competitive dynamics in ways that proved right — and that changed how others in the sector thought about the landscape.
Dimension 03

Domain Dynamics

Competitive intelligence Market positioning Business model analysis
L1 Absent
Competitor knowledge is limited to landing pages and pricing pages; business model comparisons are ad hoc or absent. Significant competitive moves are discovered on social media or from customers — not from any systematic monitoring.
L2 Emerging Baseline
Tracks key competitors' main features and pricing and is building a more systematic view, though coverage is patchy. Business model differences are recognised — "they're usage-based, we're seat-based" — but not yet analysed for what they imply about each company's constraints.
L3 Developing Target
Tracks a defined competitive set using a consistent framework — feature matrices, positioning maps, a competitor log — and updates it when moves happen. Responds to competitive shifts with a reasoned view but is still primarily reactive: the news arrives, then the analysis follows.
L4 Strong
Maintains a current view of competitive dynamics that includes not just what competitors are shipping but what they're likely to ship next, based on their hiring patterns, pricing changes, and customer complaints. Can model what a competitor's business model constrains them from doing, and uses this to identify whitespace and sharpen differentiation.
L5 Exceptional
Reads competitive dynamics at a structural level — tracking effects others miss, such as recognising that a competitor's dependence on a key platform partner would prevent them from moving upmarket before they announced it, or that a feature trend was reshaping category-wide customer expectations. Their analysis frameworks or published views are referenced by others as setting the standard.
Dimension 04

Emergent Trends

Foresight Technology scanning Design literacy
L1 Absent
Trend awareness is limited to mainstream tech news encountered by chance; shifts in technology, design paradigms, and business models are noticed only after wide adoption — well past the point of strategic relevance. Has no practice of actively scanning signals outside immediate work.
L2 Emerging Baseline
Follows a small set of sources and is building awareness of signals outside their immediate category. Can name trends that feel relevant but struggles to separate meaningful signal from noise, or to connect emerging shifts — a new interaction paradigm, a change in how infrastructure is priced — to concrete implications for their product.
L3 Developing
Maintains a defined list of 10–15 sources reviewed on a schedule — newsletters, research communities, practitioners — and keeps a running log where signals are noted and updated. Can connect some trends to product opportunities with reasonable confidence across technology, design, and business model shifts, but the pattern recognition isn't yet reliable enough to act on consistently.
L4 Strong Target
Translates signals across technology, design paradigm shifts, and business model change into concrete scenarios or near-term bets — and has a track record of being early. Has made a product or technology decision 12–18 months before the shift became obvious, and the reasoning held up; foresight actively informs what gets explored, built, or deprioritised.
L5 Exceptional
Recognised by peers as an early signal detector in their domain — their written or spoken trend framings circulate and others build strategies around them. Produces original synthesis across technology, design, and business model change that isn't easily replicated; not just consuming signals but generating frameworks others use to interpret them.
Dimension 05

Context Architecture agent-native

Brief writing Knowledge design Context structuring
L1 Absent
Relies on ad hoc prompting with minimal context; agents regularly produce output that requires substantial rewriting — and the problem is attributed to the model rather than the brief. Every session starts from scratch with no shared information layer.
L2 Emerging
Aware that agents need structured context and has started writing more detailed briefs; output quality improves with effort but context lives in long chat threads or one-off documents rather than anything reusable. Each session still requires significant setup before useful work can happen.
L3 Developing Baseline
Has built reusable context blocks — product background, personas, constraints — that can be combined for different tasks rather than rewritten each time. Agents execute well-defined tasks reliably, but novel or ambiguous tasks still produce inconsistent output because the knowledge layer has gaps and is maintained ad hoc.
L4 Strong Target
Maintains a structured, regularly updated knowledge layer — product context, personas, principles, domain knowledge — that agents draw from reliably across varied tasks. Briefs are designed so that when context is incomplete, the agent surfaces the gap rather than filling it with a plausible-sounding assumption; a new team member or agent could be onboarded using the written context alone, without a verbal walkthrough.
L5 Exceptional
Has developed original frameworks for structuring agent context that others study and adopt — published, taught, or referenced as a model. Can design an information architecture for a new domain from first principles with speed and precision; their approach is what others use as a starting point when they don't know how to begin.
